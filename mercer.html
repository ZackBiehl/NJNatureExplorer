Mercer County, home of the states capital, features mainly expansive woodlands with the acception of a bodies of water and the Delaware River.

Mercer County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Burlington County, Monmouth County, Middlesex County, Hunterdon, and Somerset County.

Climate

Mercer County has a Humid Continental Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 86°F in July to about 24°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. Since Mercer County is inland it is extremely rare for hurricanes or tropical storms to ever make it to the county.

Wildlife

Mercer County has a range of wildlife adapted to its extensive woodlands and scattered lates and rivers, making Mercer County a gateway to the Appalachians

Mammals

Mercer County provides its mammals habitats through parks, green spaces, and urban and suburban areas. These areas are home to common species like squirrels, raccoons, and white-tailed deer. Natural reserves and woodlands in the county provide habitats for a more extensive range of mammals, including foxes, opossums, various small rodents, and very occasional bobcats wandering away from the Appalachians.

Eastern Gray Squirrel, Northern Yellow Bat, Eastern Raccoon, Coyote

Birds

Mercer County boasts a vibrant avian population, with diverse bird species thriving in its varied landscapes. Urban and suburban areas attract common species such as sparrows, robins, and pigeons, while parks and natural reserves, including the Delaware River and Mercer Meadows, provide habitats for a wide array of migratory and resident birds.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Canada Goose, Cedar Waxwing, Pileated Woodpecker, Indigo Bunting

Aquatic and Marine Life

Mercer County, being inland and have less than 2% of the area being water, does not have much space for aquatic life. However being situated next to the Delaware Rivers and with the few scattered lakes around the area you can expect to see various bass, catfish, perch and trout!

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Flathead Catfish, Blue-spotted Sunfish, Margined Madtom, White Sucker

Insects and Arachnids

Mercer County is home to a diverse array of insects and arachnids. Urban and suburban areas host common species such as bees, butterflies, and spiders, while natural reserves and parks offer habitats for a wide variety of insect life. From dragonflies near freshwater bodies to the ants on forest floors, Mercer County offers a diverse look into the world of insects and arachnids

Pictures from left to right starting at top left

Amphibians and Reptiles

Mercer County supports various amphibians and reptiles in its urban, suburban, and natural landscapes. From frogs and salamanders near water sources to turtles and snakes in wooded areas, these creatures can include painted turtles, wood frogs, long-tailed salamanders, lizards, various snakes and more!

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Mercer County's flora showcases a mix of urban greenery, suburban gardens, and natural landscapes. Common trees such as oaks and maples line streets, while parks like Mercer Meadows and Rosedale Park feature diverse plant life. Native species like the flowering dogwood and tulip tree add vibrancy to the county's environment

Fungi

In Mercer County, a variety of fungi contribute to the ecological tapestry of its woodlands and natural areas. Parks like Baldpate Mountain and Mercer Meadows host species such as Amanita and oyster mushrooms. These fungi play essential roles in nutrient cycling, supporting the health of the local ecosystems.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Mercer County is crisscrossed by a network of rivers, creeks, and dotted with lakes, contributing to its diverse water landscape. The Delaware River, forming the western boundary, is a prominent feature offering recreational opportunities and scenic beauty. Local creeks, such as Stony Brook, and lakes like Mercer Lake provide additional freshwater ecosystems, supporting aquatic life and creating appealing environments for residents and visitors alike

Picture is of the Mercer Lake

Woodlands

Mercer County features expansive woodlands that enhance its natural charm and ecological diversity. Parks like Baldpate Mountain and Mercer Meadows feature dense tree cover, creating habitats for various flora and fauna. These woodlands offer a haven for hikers, birdwatchers, and nature enthusiasts, providing serene trails and glimpses of diverse plant life
