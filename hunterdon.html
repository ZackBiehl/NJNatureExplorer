Hunterdon County

Hunterdon County features exapansive woodlands and various bodies of water that provide habit for many organisms creating a diverse ecosystem.

Hunterdon County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Somerset County, Morris County, Warren County, and Mercer County.

Climate

Hunterdon County has a Humid Continental Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 85°F in July to about 19°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. Since Hunterdon County is inland it is extremely rare for hurricanes or tropical storms to ever make it to the county

Wildlife

Hunterdon County hosts a wide arrange of wildlife that lives within its woodland and aquatic environment.

Mammals

Hunterdon County is home to a variety of mammals well-adapted to its diverse landscapes. Common species in woodland areas include the Eastern red fox, white-tailed deer, and the opossum. The woodlands also provide habitats for smaller mammals like the Eastern chipmunk and various bat species. Additionally, mammals such as minks and river otters can be found in the county's water bodies.

Birds

In Hunterdon County, a diverse array of birds graces its varied landscapes. Common species include the Northern cardinal, American robin, and red-tailed hawk, thriving in wooded areas and suburban settings. The woodlands provide habitats for smaller birds like the Black-capped Chickadee and various warbler species. Additionally, water bodies within the county support waterfowl such as mallards and great blue herons.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Eastern Phoebe, Northern Saw-whet Owl, Pine Siskin, Sharp-shinned Hawk

Aquatic and Marine Life

Hunterdon County is home to a variety of marine and freshwater life contributes to its ecological richness. Freshwater environments host fish species like the brook trout, largemouth bass, and sunfish. Hunterdon County's freshwater ecosystems play a crucial role in supporting diverse aquatic life and providing habitats for different species.

Insects and Arachnids

Hunterdon County hosts a diverse array of insects and arachnids enriches its ecosystems. Common insects include butterflies like the Eastern tiger swallowtail and dragonflies such as the Common Green Darner. Arachnids like the garden spider are often found in wooded areas, contributing to the local biodiversity.

Amphibians and Reptiles

Hunterdon County provides different habitats to a variety of amphibians and reptiles. Common amphibians include the Eastern American toad and the Northern green frog, found in swampy areas. Reptiles like the Eastern garter snake and Eastern box turtle thrive in a range of habitats, from woodlands to suburban gardens. These species play crucial roles in the local ecosystem, contributing to insect control and maintaining a balance in the food chain.

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Hunterdon County features a diverse array of flora living in the countys diverse environments. Common deciduous trees include the Red Maple and Tulip Tree, while the Eastern Redbud adds a splash of color in spring. Native wildflowers such as the Black-eyed Susan and Goldenrod bloom in open spaces, supporting pollinators. Additionally, swampy areas host species like the Buttonbush, enhancing biodiversity.

Fungi

In Hunterdon County, various fungi contribute to its ecosystems. Mushrooms like the Eastern American Jack-O'-Lantern and Splitgill Mushroom can be found in woodlands, while the Chanterelle and Morel mushrooms add to the fungal diversity. These fungi contribute to decomposition, recycling nutrients back into the soil.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Hunterdon County is graced by waterways such as the Delaware River, Neshanic River, and lakes like Lake Solitude. These local features not only enhance the county's scenic appeal but also provide habitats for diverse aquatic life within Hunterdon County while also providing recreation for residents.

Woodlands

Hunterdon County's woodlands, including those in areas like the Teetertown Ravine Nature Preserve and Voorhees State Park, harbor a diverse array of trees such as the towering Red Oak, majestic Tulip Tree, and resilient Eastern Hemlock. Various fungi and flora inhabit the countys expansive woodlands along with many organisms big and small. Hunterdon County's woodlands offer habit to many different lifeforms and provide residents with recreation and a connection to nature.

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • Ken Lockwood Gorge Wildlife Management Area - 563 acres filled with small waterfalls, scenic trails and great fishing spots! (fish responsibly)
  • Round valley Recreation Area - 2,350 acres great for boating, camping, fishing, picnicking, and lifeguarded swimming, as well as over 30 miles of trails!
  • Mine Brook Park - Great for kids, short hiking trails and playgrounds!
  • Hoffman Park - Short trails around the grasslands and 32 ponds!
  • Horseshoe Bend Park - Over 17 miles of trails!
  • Miquin Woods Preserve - A few short, easy and enjoyable trails!
  • Sourland Mountain Preserve - Over 35 miles of trails through a preserve that also includes portions of Mercer County and Somerset County
  • Teetertown Ravine Nature Preserve - Over 300 acres of natual area that provide trails, cross-country skiing, horseback riding and more!
  • Voorhees State Park - 1,400 acres that include fishing, swimming, over 8.5 miles of trails, great camping spots and more!
  • Bernadette Morales Nature Preserve - Small waterfalls and scenic trails!
