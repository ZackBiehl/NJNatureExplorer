Monmouth County features a dynamic mix of coastal, woodland, and suburban environments, creating a diverse ecosystem for a range of plant and animal species.

Monmouth County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Burlington County, Mercer County, Middlesex County, and Ocean County.

Climate

Monmouth County, along the Atlantic Ocean, has a Humid Subtropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 86°F in July to about 22°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths

Wildlife

Monmouth County's wildlife thrives in its varied ecosystems, with coastal habitats, woodlands, and suburban areas supporting a diverse array of organisms

Mammals

Monmouth County's woodlands, suburban areas, and parks harbor a diverse range of wildlife, featuring species like white-tailed deer, gray squirrels, raccoons, and red foxes. These environments, enriched by native trees such as oaks and maples, offer habitats for various animals, providing residents and visitors alike with opportunities to observe and appreciate the county's vibrant fauna amidst urban and natural settings.

This section excludes marine mammals which can be found in the aquatic and marine life section.

Birds

Monmouth County's birdlife flourishes in its diverse landscapes, with common sightings including red-tailed hawks soaring in the skies, robins and cardinals gracing suburban gardens, and waterfowl like ducks and geese inhabiting local waterways. The county's mix of coastal areas, woodlands, and suburban environments creates a habitat mosaic that supports a rich avian community

Aquatic and Marine Life

In Monmouth County common marine species in the area include various fish like striped bass and bluefish. The area is also known to have visits from marine mammals such as bottlenose dolphins and whales like the humpback whale. The diverse range of marine life creates a coastal culture for residents and other wildlife to enjoy.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
White Mullet, Striped Burrfish, Humpback Whale, Atlantic Menhaden

Insects and Arachnids

Monmouth County hosts a diverse range of insects and arachnids, contributing to the region's ecological tapestry. In the warm months, meadows and gardens come alive with the vibrant colors of butterflies like the Eastern Tiger Swallowtail and the Monarch. Meanwhile, arachnids such as orb-weaving spiders and wolf spiders thrive in the county's varied landscapes, playing crucial roles in controlling insect populations.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Common Buckeye, Yellow Garden Spider, Calico Pennant, North American Wheel Bug

Amphibians and Reptiles

Monmouth County boasts a diverse array of amphibians and reptiles within its varied ecosystems. Common amphibians in the region include the Northern Leopard Frog and the American Bullfrog, often found near freshwater habitats such as ponds and marshes. Reptiles, on the other hand, thrive in the county's woodlands and meadows. Species like the Eastern Box Turtle and Eastern Garter Snake are frequently encountered

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Monmouth County is adorned with a diverse array of flora, showcasing the region's rich botanical heritage. Deciduous forests within the county are home to a variety of trees such as oaks, maples, and beech, providing a vibrant display of colors during the fall season. Along the coastal areas, salt-tolerant plants like beach plum and seaside goldenrod thrive, contributing to the resilience of the local ecosystemsWith its mix of woodlands and coastal environments.

Fungi

In Monmouth County, fungi, like turkey tail and bracket fungi, play crucial roles in ecosystem functions. They contribute to nutrient cycling, decomposer species aid in breaking down organic matter, and overall, these fungi contribute to maintaining a healthy and balanced environment.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Monmouth County is traversed and spotted with rivers, creeks, and lakes that contribute significantly to the region's natural beauty and ecological health. The Navesink and Shrewsbury Rivers meander through the landscape, providing vital habitats for aquatic life and serving as scenic waterways for recreational activities. Likewise, lakes like Lake Assunpink provide habitat for various organisms.

Picture is of the Lake Assunpink

Woodlands

Monmouth County features extensive woodlands that support diverse ecosystems and provide recreational spaces. These forests, like those in Hartshorne Woods Park and Allaire State Park, offer habitats for various species and serve as natural retreats for hikers. Beyond their scenic charm, these woodlands play a vital role in maintaining environmental balance, contributing to air and water quality in Monmouth County.

Beaches

Monmouth County's coastal beaches, exemplified by Sandy Hook and Asbury Park, play a crucial role in supporting local ecosystems. These sandy shores provide habitats for various coastal wildlife, contributing to the region's biodiversity. Additionally, the beaches help protect the area from flooding and storm surges that may occur. Some of these Beaches include, Sandy Hook, Asbury Park, Long Branch Beach, belmar Beach, Spring Lake Beach, Sea Bright Beach, Avon-by-the-Sea Beach, Bradley Beach, Monmouth Beach and Ideal beach.
