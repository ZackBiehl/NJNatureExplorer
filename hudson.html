Hudson County, in between two rivers, is mostly made up of urban areas, however, the scattered green spaces and the river and bay areas support a suprisingly diverse ecosystem.

Hudson County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Essex County and Bergen County.

Climate

Hudson County has a Humid Subropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 84°F in July to about 27°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths

Wildlife

Essex County is home to diverse wildlife, these species can find a home in the ecosystems in the woodlands or in the various water bodies..

Mammals

Hudson County hosts a variety of mammals, including the Eastern gray squirrel and white-tailed deer in urban and suburban areas. Along the Hudson River, the North American river otter is a sleek presence, while nocturnal species like the little brown bat aid in insect control. Adaptable creatures like the Eastern cottontail rabbit and raccoon thrive in both urban and natural environments.

This section excludes marine mammals which can be found in the aquatic and marine life section.

Birds

Hudson County's bird population includes Eastern bluebirds, American robins, Canada geese, mallards, peregrine falcons, red-tailed hawks, great egrets, and snowy egrets. This variety of species adapts to both urban, natural and aquatic environments, offering a diverse and dynamic birdwatching experience in the region.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
American Woodcock, Horned Grebe, Double-crested Cormorant, Snowy Owl

Aquatic and Marine Life

Hudson County's aquatic and marine life includes a diverse array of species. Along the Hudson River, you can find various fish such as striped bass and bluefish. The river also hosts crustaceans like blue crabs. Additionally, marine mammals like bottlenose dolphins occasionally venture into the river and can be seen in the Upper New York Bay, adding a unique aspect to the region's aquatic ecosystem.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Atlantic Grey Seal, Harbour Porpoise, Shortnose Sturgeon, Oyster Toadfish

Insects and Arachnids

Hudson County hosts a diverse collective of insects and arachnids. Common sightings include butterflies, bees, and ants. Various spider species weave intricate webs, contributing to the local insect population control. The region also hosts dragonflies, grasshoppers, and beetles, showcasing the rich biodiversity of insects in both urban and natural settings.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Differential Grasshopper, Ground Wolf Spider, Sycamore Tussock Moth, Long-tailed Giant Icheneumonid Wasp

Amphibians and Reptiles

Hudson County's amphibian and reptile population includes species adapted to its diverse landscapes. This area is home to frogs, toads, and salamanders, which thrive in aquatic habitats. Additionally, reptiles such as turtles and snakes can be found in both urban and suburban environments. These Organisms help control insect populations and add to the countys biodiversity.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Hudson County's flora comprises a variety of plant species, adapting to both urban and suburban settings. Common trees include oak, maple, and pine, providing greenery in parks and along streets. Shrubs such as holly and azalea add to the landscape's diversity. Urban gardens and natural areas showcase a mix of flowering plants like daisies and goldenrod.

Fungi

Hudson County hosts a variety of fungi, contributing to its ecological diversity. Common examples include mushrooms, molds, and yeasts. These organisms play essential roles in decomposition and nutrient cycling within the region.

Environments

Hudson County, while has some green spaces, is mainly an urban area with the acception of some lakes and the rivers that it the county is in between.

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Hudson County is in between the famous Hudson River and the Hackensack River. The Hackensack River also has an assortment of marshes at the Mill Creek Marsh. The county also has various creeks and lakes scattered and traversing the region, contributing to recreation for residents and providing habitats for many organisms big and small.
