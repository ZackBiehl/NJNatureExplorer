Ocean County, being the biggest county in New Jersey at 914.84 square miles, has an extensive mix of environments which provide habitats for diverse wildlife, creating a big and interesting ecosystem.

Ocean County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Atlantic County, Burlington County, and Monmouth County.

Climate

Ocean County, along the Atlantic Ocean, has a Humid Subtropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 85°F in July to about 24°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage to the coast of Ocean County.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths. Severe thunderstorms are common during the warm season. In the Pine Barrens it is not uncommon for radiative cooling to occur which is the process by which a body loses heat by thermal radiation. Due to sandy soil, on clear and dry nights these areas might be 10 to 15 °F colder than the surrounding areas.

Wildlife

Ocean County is home to a diverse wildlife population, featuring a variety of terrestrial and aquatic species adapted to its coastal and inland ecosystems.

Mammals

Ocean County supports a variety of mammals adapted to its diverse landscapes. Common sightings include white-tailed deer, eastern cottontail rabbits, raccoons, and gray squirrels. Coastal areas may host marine mammals, such as seals, at times. These mammals contribute to the county's terrestrial and aquatic biodiversity.

This section excludes marine mammals which can be found in the aquatic and marine life section.

Birds

Ocean County offers a rich avian diversity with common bird species including ospreys, egrets, seagulls, and various songbirds. The county's coastal and inland environments, including wetlands and woodlands, provide vital habitats for resident and migratory birds, making it a popular destination for birdwatchers.

Boat-tailed Grackle, Herring Gull, Red-Throated Loon, Ruby-throated Hummingbird

Aquatic and Marine Life

Ocean County boasts a diverse aquatic and marine life. Common sightings include bottlenose dolphins, bluefish, flounder, and various species of crabs. The county's coastal and estuarine environments contribute to a vibrant marine ecosystem, attracting marine enthusiasts and supporting local fisheries.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left

Insects and Arachnids

Ocean County showcases a captivating array of insects and arachnids, ranging from butterflies and bees to dragonflies and diverse spiders. The county's diverse environments, including coastal areas, woodlands, and marshes offering habitats for rich insect and arachnid biodiversity.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left

Amphibians and Reptiles

Ocean County supports a diverse community of amphibians and reptiles. Common sightings include frogs, such as the Spring Peeper, Eastern American Toad, and various snake species like the Northern Water Snake. The county's wetlands, woodlands, and suburban areas provide crucial habitats for these fascinating creatures, contributing to the county's diversity.

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Ocean County boasts a diverse flora, featuring coastal vegetation like beach grasses and seaside goldenrod, along with woodlands adorned with oak and pine trees. Blueberry bushes thrive in certain areas, adding to the county's botanical richness. These varied plant communities contribute to the ecological vibrancy of Ocean County.

Fungi

Ocean County is home to a variety of fungi, including mushrooms and molds. These organisms play an essential role in decomposition and nutrient cycling in the county's diverse ecosystems, contributing to the overall health of woodlands, wetlands, and other natural environments.

Environments

Beaches

Ocean County's beaches along the Atlantic coast offer sandy shores, dunes, and coastal vegetation, providing picturesque landscapes and recreational spaces. These coastal environments are vital components of the county's ecosystem, protecting the region from flooding and storm surges. These beaches attract residents and visitors alike to enjoy the sun, surf, and natural beauty. These beaches include, Brick Township beaches, Island Beach State Park, Jenkinson's Beach, Lavallatte beach, Long Beach Island, Ocean County Parks, Ortley Beach, Seaside Heights Beach.

Picture is of Surf City Beach

Woodlands

Ocean County's woodlands present a tapestry of natural beauty, featuring a variety of tree species like oak and pine. These woodlands create vital habitats for wildlife and offer residents and visitors serene escapes into nature. The county's wooded areas contribute to its ecological diversity and provide opportunities for outdoor recreation.

Picture is of Double Trouble State Park

Wetlands

Ocean County's wetlands, including expansive salt marshes and estuaries, play a crucial role in supporting diverse flora and fauna. These wetland ecosystems contribute to the county's environmental health, providing habitats for various species, acting as natural filters, and enhancing the overall resilience of the region's coastal environment.

Rivers, Creeks, and Lakes

Ocean County is traversed by rivers such as the Toms River, contributing to the county's diverse water ecosystems. Various lakes in the county also provide homes for many organisms. These waterways support aquatic life and provide recreational opportunities for residents, adding to the county's natural beauty and environmental richness.
