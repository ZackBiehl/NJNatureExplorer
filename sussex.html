Sussex County hosts diverse ecosystems including expanisve woodlands, various water bodies, and breataking mountain ranges, providing habitats to a wide array of wildlife and plants.

Sussex County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Warren County, Morris County and Passaic County.

Climate

Sussex County has a Humid Continetal Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 82°F in July to about 16°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. Since Sussex County is inland it is extremely rare for hurricanes or tropical storms to ever make it to the county

Wildlife

Sussex County is home to a wide array of wildlife inhabiting expansive woodlands, lakes, other bodies of water, and breathtaking mountain ranges.

Mammals

Sussex County is home to a diverse population of mammals that inhabit various environments, featuring prominent species such as the White-tailed Deer, Eastern Coyotes and bobcats. In addition to these, the county hosts smaller yet significant mammals like the Eastern Chipmunk, Red Fox, and Raccoon.

Birds

Sussex County offers a diverse avian landscape, with woodlands hosting songbirds like the American Robin and Northern Cardinal, bodies of watter attracting waterfowl such as the Mallard and Great Blue Heron, and open spaces providing habitats for raptors like the Red-tailed Hawk and Turkey Vulture. This wide range of avian wildlife makes Sussex County an amazing spot for birdwatching!

Aquatic and Marine Life

Sussex County hosts a diverse array of aquatic and marine life, particularly in its freshwater ecosystems. The Delaware River and local water bodies are home to various fish species, including Smallmouth Bass, Catfish, Brook Trout, and Sunfish.

Insects and Arachnids

Sussex County's insect and arachnid community is diverse, showcasing pollinators like the Honeybee and Monarch Butterfly in meadows, while woodlands are inhabited by industrious ants, ground beetles, and arachnids like the orb-weaving Garden Spider Aquatic areas also attract dragonflies like the blue dasher and the widow skimmer.

Amphibians and Reptiles

Sussex County hosts a variety of amphibians and reptiles, adding to its rich biodiversity. Aquatic areas like the Wallkill River National Wildlife Refuge provide suitable habitats for amphibians such as Eastern Red-backed Salamanders and Northern Leopard Frogs. Reptiles like Eastern Garter Snakes and Eastern Box Turtles are also found in diverse ecosystems within the county. These species play crucial roles in the local ecosystem, contributing to insect control and maintaining a balance in the food chain.

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Sussex County boasts a diverse array of flora, contributing to its vibrant ecosystems. Wooded areas like Stokes State Forest feature a mix of tree species, including Red Maple and Eastern Hemlock. Wildflowers such as Trillium and Jack-in-the-Pulpit can also be spotted in the county's natural landscapes. The flora in Sussex County plays a crucial role in supporting local wildlife and maintaining the ecological health of the area.

Pictures from left to right
Canadian Lousewort, Wild Basil

Fungi

Sussex County is home to a variety of fungi, contributing to the region's fungal diversity. In the woodlands and forests, you can find species like the Chanterelle mushroom and the Turkey Tail fungus These species contribute to the local ecosystem through decomposition, recycling nutrients back into the soil.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Sussex County boasts a network of rivers, creeks, and lakes that enhance its aquatic diversity. Bodies of water such as the Paulins Kill River, Wallkill River, and Lake Mohawk contribute to the county's rich aquatic ecosystems. These waterways support a variety of aquatic life, including fish species like trout and bass, as well as provide recreational opportunities to residents.

Picture is of Crater Lake

Woodlands

Sussex County features multiple expansive woodlands throughout the county. Notable woodlands, including Stokes State Forest, High Point State Park, and Kittatinny Valley State Park, are vital components of the county's natural landscape. These wooded areas support a variety of plant and animal species, contributing to the overall biodiversity. Residents can explore these woodlands to experience the tranquility and beauty of Sussex County's nature.

Picture is of Stokes State Forest

Mountains

The Kittatinny Ridge, part of the Appalachian Mountains, traverses the county, parts of this ridge include High Point, the highest point in the state of New Jersey, other notable sections are Sunrise Mountain, Culver's Lake, Swartswood State Park, and more. These ranges indlude woodlands and bodies of water, providing habitat for a diverse range of wildlife. These ranges also give residents recreational opportunities to connect with nature as well as enjoy skiing and access to the Appalachians!

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • High Point State Park - 1,803 feet above sea level, with 14,218 acres of woodland on Kittatinny Mountain full of nature, trails and activities!
  • Kittatinny Valley State Park - Over 60 miles of trails through diverse scenery!
  • Stokes State Forest - More than 63 miles of marked trails, great for cross-country skiing, horseback riding and more along the Appalachian Trail!
  • Swartswood State Park - Great for canoeing, kayaking, sailing, fishing (fish responsibly), swimming in the Swartswood lakes calm waters, as well as enjoyable hikes!
  • Wawayanda State Park - Spanning over 34,000 acres with 21 trails ranging from 2.6 miles to 16.6 miles, this is a combonation of both Passaic County and Sussex County!
  • Lafayette Pond Park - Trails around the pond and open space for all sorts of activites!
  • Allamuchy Mountain State Park - Over 34 miles of trails in the over 9,000 acres of land, also apart of Warren County!
