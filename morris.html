Morris County has diverse ecosystems including woodlands, various waterways and lake, providing a habitat to a rich array of flora.

Morris County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Somerset County, Essex County, Union County, Hunterdon County, Passais County, Warren County, and Sussex County.

Climate

Morris County has a Humid Continental Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 85°F in July to about 18°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. Since Morris County is inland it is extremely rare for hurricanes or tropical storms to ever make it to the county

Wildlife

Morris County features diverse wildlife that lives within its woodland, urban and aquatic environments.

Mammals

Morris County is home to a diverse range of mammals adapted to its varied landscapes. Common species include the Eastern gray squirrel, white-tailed deer, and raccoon, which thrive in suburban and wooded areas. The woodlands also host smaller mammals such as chipmunks and cottontail rabbits. Additionally, various bat species contribute to the local ecosystem by aiding in insect control during the nighttime.

American Black Bear, Bobcat, Common Raccoon, Red Fox

Birds

Morris County features a variety of birds, from familiar ones like robins and bluebirds to raptors like red-tailed hawks. Suburban areas attract common songbirds, while lakes and ponds host waterfowl such as ducks and geese. The regions diverse avian population makes Morris County a great area birdwatching near rivers, lakes and in woodlands.

Aquatic and Marine Life

Morris County's aquatic and marine life primarily centers around its freshwater environments. Streams, rivers, and lakes within the county support a variety of fish species, including largemouth bass, brown trout, and bluegill sunfish.

Insects and Arachnids

In Morris County, you'll find the Eastern Tiger Swallowtail butterfly, honey bees, and Eastern Black Carpenter Ants. Common spiders include the Cross Orbweaver and various Jumping Spiders. Dragonflies like the Common Green Darner and grasshoppers like the Two-striped Grasshopper add to Morris Countys insect diversity.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left

Amphibians and Reptiles

In Morris County, you can encounter amphibians such as the Northern Leopard Frog and Eastern Red-backed Salamander. Freshwater environments may host turtles like the Painted Turtle. Reptiles include the Eastern Garter Snake, commonly found in diverse habitats, and the Eastern Box Turtle, often spotted in wooded areas. These species play crucial roles in the local ecosystem, contributing to insect control and maintaining a balance in the food chain.

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Morris County's plant life includes trees like the Red Oak and Sugar Maple, suburban ornamentals such as Rhododendron and Azalea, and native wildflowers like Black-Eyed Susan and Goldenrod and trees like the Eastern Red Cedar are common in drier areas.

Fungi

Morris County is home to fungi like the Fly Agaric and Chicken-of-the-Woods. Mushrooms, including the Chanterelle and morel, contribute to the fungal diversity in the region These fungi contribute to decomposition, recycling nutrients back into the soil.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Union County features a network of rivers, creeks, and lakes that enhance its landscape. The Rahway River flows through scenic areas, offering recreational opportunities and habitat for organisms. Various creeks weave through urban and natural spaces. Lakes, like those in Echo Lake Park and Warinanco Park, provide homes for all types of organisms and recreational activities for residents.

Picture is of the Echo Lake in Echo Lake Park

Woodlands

Morris County's woodlands, like those in Mahlon Dickerson Reservation, feature a mix of Red Oak, Sugar Maple, and Eastern Red Cedar trees. Patriots' Path and Lewis Morris County Park also include wooded areas, providing a scenic backdrop for outdoor enthusiasts These wooded areas offer habitat for wildlife and support various plant species, as well as provide areas to residents to connect, experience and learn about nature.
