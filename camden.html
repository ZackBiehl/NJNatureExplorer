Climate

Ocean County, along the Atlantic Ocean, has a Humid Subtropical Climate. Ocean County is also the biggest county in New Jersey at 914.84 square miles. With temperatures ranging from about 87°F in July to about 26°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage to the coast of Ocean County.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, since Camden County is not connected to the ocean it experiences less effects of nor'eastors and hurricanes than coastal areas in the state.

Wildlife

Camden County's wildlife is characterized by a mix of urban and natural habitats, supporting various species adapted to the county's diverse landscapes.

Mammals

Camden County hosts a variety of mammals adapted to its environments. Common sightings include white-tailed deer, eastern gray squirrels, raccoons, and opossums. Urban and suburban areas may also be inhabited by smaller mammals like rabbits and various rodent species. While larger wildlife is limited due to urbanization, the county's green spaces and parks provide habitats for these mammals, contributing to the local biodiversity.

American mink, Muskrat, Striped Skunk, Virginia Opossum

Birds

Camden County offers a diverse avian landscape with common bird species such as sparrows, robins, pigeons, and cardinals found in urban and suburban areas. Parks and green spaces may attract a variety of songbirds, while water bodies like the Delaware River may host waterfowl and other aquatic birds. While the urbanization limits some species, Camden County provides habitats for both resident and migratory birds, making it a dynamic area for birdwatching.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Canada Goose, Carolina Chickadee, Hooded Merganser, Wild turkey

Aquatic and Marine Life

Camden County, being inland and not directly connected to the ocean, has limited aquatic life compared to coastal areas. However, the Delaware River, which borders the county, supports freshwater aquatic life. Common species include various fish, such as catfish, bass, and perch. The river and its tributaries also host freshwater invertebrates. While not traditionally associated with aquatic life, these water bodies contribute to Camden County's aquatic biodiversity.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Golden Shiner, White Catfish, Yellow Bullhead, Pumpkinseed

Insects and Arachnids

Camden County is home to a diverse array of insects and arachnids. Urban and suburban areas may host common insects like ants, bees, and butterflies. Parks and green spaces provide habitats for dragonflies, grasshoppers, and various spiders. While urbanization can impact certain species, Camden County's varied landscapes offer environments for a rich insect and arachnid biodiversity.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Eastern Yellowjacket, Fragile Forktail, Orchard Orbweaver, Polyphemus Moth

Amphibians and Reptiles

Camden County, with its mix of urban, suburban, and green spaces, supports a variety of amphibians and reptiles. Common sightings include frogs like the Northern Green Frog, Eastern American Toad, and painted turtles and various snake species. Green spaces, parks, and water bodies provide crucial habitats for these creatures, contributing to the county's herpetological diversity. While urbanization poses challenges, efforts to preserve natural areas are in effect to offer habitats for these organisms and to increase biodiversity.

Urban areas feature ornamental plants and street trees, while parks and green spaces may showcase native wildflowers and grasses.

Camden County hosts a variety of fungi, including mushrooms and molds, contributing to its ecological diversity. These organisms play a crucial role in nutrient cycling and decomposition within the county's varied landscapes, from urban areas to green spaces.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and Lakes

Camden County is traversed by the Delaware River, a significant waterway that influences the county's landscapes. The river provides habitats for various aquatic species and supports recreational activities. Additionally, smaller rivers and creeks, such as the Cooper River, contribute to the county's diverse waterways. Various lakes in the county also provide homes for many organisms. These bodies of water play a vital role in shaping the local environment, offering scenic views, and providing opportunities for outdoor recreation along their shores.

Woodlands

Camden County, despite its urban and suburban character, features pockets of woodlands and green spaces. These woodlands, including areas like Cooper River Park and scattered urban forests, provide a refuge for native trees such as oaks, maples, and pines. While smaller in scale compared to more rural regions, these wooded areas contribute to the county's ecological diversity, offering residents and visitors a connection to nature within the urban environment.

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • Timber Creek Park - Dog park with 9 acres of fenced in woods as well as other trails!
  • Atco Lake - 77 acres of land with natural trials and beautiful sights!
  • Berlin Park - Over 5 miles of trails around the Great Egg Harbor River, also many sports fields and courts!
  • Haddon Lake Park - Great for kids, bike paths, playground and sports fields!
  • New Brooklyn Park - Over 750 acres of land with the beautiful New Brooklyn Lake, also has sports fields and a disc golf course!
  • New Camden Park - Located adjacent to the Cooper River, while small, has a lot of space in an open field and calming walking trails!
  • Newton Lake Park - Great for picnics!
  • Cooper River Park - Over 4 miles of bike path and an awesome Sculpture Garden as well as the Jack Curtis Stadium!
  • Wharton State Forest - About 40 miles of hiking, located in Camden, Atlantic, and Burlington Counties, This is apart of the Pinelands National Reserve!
