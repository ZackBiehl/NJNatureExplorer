Cumberland County encompasses a diverse environment blending woodlands, wetlands, agricultural areas, and urban spaces, fostering a rich ecosystem that supports varied flora and fauna.

Cumberland County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Cape May County, Atlantic County, and Salem County

Climate

Cumberland County has a Humid Subtropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 87°F in July to about 25°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths

Wildlife

Cumberland County's wildlife is marked by a diverse array of species adapted to its woodlands, wetlands, and agricultural landscapes

Mammals

Cumberland County hosts a diverse array of mammals reflecting its varied ecosystems. Among the notable residents are the white-tailed deer, Eastern cottontail rabbit, The Eastern coyote, the North American raccoon, and more. These organisms adapt to both urban and rural environments. showcasing its ecological richness and adaptability.

This section excludes marine mammals which can be found in the aquatic and marine life section.

Birds

Cumberland County's avian population is characterized by a variety of species adapted to its diverse landscapes, with common sightings including raptors such as red-tailed hawks, waterfowl like mallards, and songbirds such as robins and cardinals. The county's woodlands, wetlands, and urban areas contribute to the rich diversity of birdlife.

Aquatic and Marine Life

Cumberland County, situated along the Delaware Bay, hosts diverse aquatic life, featuring various fish species like striped bass and bluefish. The bay is also frequented by marine mammals such as bottlenose dolphins, adding to the richness of Cumberland County's coastal ecosystem.

Insects and Arachnids

Cumberland County is home to a diverse range of insects and arachnids. Across all of the countys landscapes, being agricultural areas, Woodlands, Wetlands, and urban areas, these organisms are able to find a home. With common insects including the Black Swallotail, various moths and butterflies, spiders ranging from small spiders like the Bold Jumping Spider to bigger spiders like the Rabid Wolf Spider. The wetlands attract many dragon flies like the Slaty Skimmer, Blue Dasher, and more!

Amphibians and Reptiles

Cumberland County is home to a diverse range of amphibians and reptiles, contributing to the area's ecological richness. Common amphibians such as the Eastern American Toad and the Northern Green Frog thrive in wetland habitats and reptiles like the Eastern Box Turtle and the Northern Black Racer inhabit wooded areas and open fields. Conservation efforts to protect their habitats are plaed to ensure the preservation of Cumberland County's unique biodiversity.

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Cumberland County boasts a varied and vibrant flora that adds to the county's natural beauty. Diverse ecosystems, including woodlands, wetlands, and urban areas, host a rich array of plant species. In wooded areas, one can find the native Red Maple and Eastern Hemlock while the Wetlands are adorned with species like the Skunk Cabbage. Ubran Areas are spotted with wild flowers and common street trees.

Pictures from left to right
Cardinal Flower, Spotted Horse mint

Fungi

Cumberland County supports a diverse array of fungi across its woodlands and wetlands. Species such as the Fly Agaric and Turkey Tail thrive in wooded areas, contributing to decomposition processes. Wetlands host fungi like the Swamp Death Angel, aiding in organic matter breakdown. The conservation of these fungi is vital for preserving ecological balance and the overall health of Cumberland County's diverse ecosystems.These organisms play a crucial role in nutrient cycling and decomposition within the county's varied landscapes, from urban areas to green spaces.

Bushy Beard Lichen, Orange Jelly Spot

Environments

Beaches

Cumberland County features a network of waterways like the Maurice River, Menantico Creek, and Union Lake. These diverse aquatic ecosystems contribute to the county's natural beauty and offer recreational opportunities. Preserving these rivers, creeks, and lakes is essential for maintaining clean water and the overall ecological balance of Cumberland County.

Picture is of the Lake Audrey

Woodlands

Cumberland County boasts extensive woodlands, including the distinctive Pine Barrens with Pitch Pine and Blueberry, as well as hardwood forests featuring White Oak and American Beech. These ecosystems support a rich variety of flora and fauna, emphasizing the importance of conservation for maintaining the natural diversity and ecological balance of the county's woodlands.
