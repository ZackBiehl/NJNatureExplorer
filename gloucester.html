Gloucester County has a blend of farmland and green spaces along the Delaware River which provide habitats for a variety of wildlife.

Gloucester County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Atlantic County, Camden County, and Salem County

Climate

Gloucester County has a Humid Subtropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 87°F in July to about 26°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage to the coast of Ocean County.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, since Gloucester County is not connected to the ocean it experiences less effects of nor'eastors and hurricanes than coastal areas in the state.

Wildlife

Gloucester County's wildlife showcases a diversity of species adapted to its landscapes, including woodlands, urban, and agricultural areas

Mammals

In Gloucester County, various mammals inhabit its varied environments. With commonly observed white-tailed deer, eastern gray squirrels, raccoons, and opossums, as well as red foxes, groundhogs, and cottontail rabbits in the county's woodlands, farmlands, and suburban areas.

Birds

Gloucester County, New Jersey, offers a diverse avian landscape, with common bird species including songbirds like robins and cardinals, waterfowl such as ducks and geese, and raptors like hawks. The county's woodlands and urban areas provide habitats for a variety of resident and migratory birds, making it a notable destination for birdwatching enthusiasts.

Wood Duck, Tufted Titmouse, Snow Goose, Carolina Wren

Aquatic and Marine Life

Gloucester County, being inland and not directly connected to the ocean, aswell as only 4.4% of the county being water, has limited marine life compared to coastal areas. However, the Delaware River, which borders the county, supports freshwater aquatic life, including various fish species such as catfish, bass, and perch.

Insects and Arachnids

Gloucester County, New Jersey, is home to a diverse array of insects and arachnids. Urban and suburban areas may host common insects like bees, butterflies, and ants, while parks and natural spaces provide habitats for dragonflies, grasshoppers, and various spider species.

Amphibians and Reptiles

Gloucester County, with its mix of urban, suburban, and natural environments, supports a variety of amphibians and reptiles. Common sightings include frogs like the Northern Green Frog, Eastern American Toad, and painted turtles, as well as various snake species. Green spaces, parks, and water bodies provide crucial habitats for these fascinating creatures.

Gloucester County showcases diverse flora across its urban, suburban, and natural landscapes. Common plant species include a variety of deciduous and evergreen trees such as oaks, maples, and pines and parks and green spaces feature native wildflowers and grasses. Despite urbanization, Gloucester County maintains a balance between developed and natural areas, fostering a diverse array of plant life.

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and Lakes

Gloucester County is traversed by various rivers and creeks, with the Delaware River being the most prominent. Additionally, smaller waterways such as Mantua Creek and Raccoon Creek contribute to the county's environment. Various lakes in the county also provide homes for many organisms. These rivers,creeks and lakes enhance the county's natural beauty, provide habitats for aquatic life, and offer recreational opportunities for residents and visitors.

Woodlands

Gloucester County's woodlands encompass a variety of natural areas, including parks, preserves, and wooded regions. Native tree species such as oaks, maples, and pines contribute to the county's wooded landscapes. These woodlands serve as habitats for diverse wildlife, offering residents and visitors peaceful retreats into nature within the urban and suburban environment.

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • Greenwich Lake Park - Great for picnics, boating, and fishing (fish responisbly)
  • Scotland Run Park - Over 1,300 acres, with great recreation, beautiful trails, boating and more!
  • Tall Pines State Preserve - A 2.6 mile loop that is great for hiking!
  • Beckett Park - A 3.2 mile loop around nature, great for biking, running or just a casual and enjoyable walk!

  • Washington Lake Park - covering over 330 acres and has great hiking trails!

  • Piney Hollow Preservation Area - A very enjoyable few miles of trails!
  • Pinelands National Reserve - Covers 22% of the state, Located in Burlington, Atlantic, Camden, Cape May, Cumberland, Gloucester and Ocean Counties
