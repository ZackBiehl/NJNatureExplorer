Middlesex County offers a diverse environment with woodlands, beaches on the Raritan Bay, and various rivers and lakes running through the region.

Middlesex County's environment and ecosystems have common characteristics with Monmouth County, Somerset County, Union County and Mercer County.

Climate

Somerset County has a Humid Continental Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 86°F in July to about 22°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain and very strong winds.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths

Wildlife

Middlesex County's wildlife is diverse, encompassing a variety of species adapted to its coastal, inland, and aquatic environments

Mammals

Middlesex County houses diverse mammals, including White-tailed Deer and Eastern Gray Squirrels in wooded areas, North American Beavers and Eastern Cottontail Rabbits near water bodies, and adaptable Eastern Coyotes in urban environments. Conservation is crucial to preserving their habitats and maintaining ecological balance in Middlesex County.

Eastern Chipmunk, Deer Mouse, White-tailed Deer, Virginia Oppssum

Birds

Middlesex County supports diverse birdlife across its landscapes. Wooded areas host songbirds like the Northern Cardinal and Wood Thrush, while wetlands attract waterfowl such as Mallards and Great Blue Herons. Raptors like the Red-tailed Hawk inhabit open spaces, and urban environments feature adaptable species like Rock Pigeons.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
White-throated Sparrow, Eastern Towhee, Northern Flicker, American Goldfinch

Aquatic and Marine Life

Middlesex County embraces diverse aquatic life in its waterways. The Raritan River hosts freshwater species like Common Carp and Bluegill, while reservoirs such as Farrington Lake support Largemouth Bass and various sunfish. The Raritan Bay fosters marine life including Striped Bass and Bluefish with the addition of marine mammals like seals and dolphins.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Common Dolphin, Harbor Seal, Red Breast Sunfish, Striped Bass

Insects and Arachnids

Middlesex County harbors a diverse community of insects and arachnids. Wooded areas host pollinators like the Eastern Tiger Swallowtail Butterfly and ants, while meadows attract bees and butterflies such as the Honeybee and Monarch. In various habitats, arachnids like the orb-weaving Garden Spider thrive.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Common Eastern Bumble Bee, Sylvan Jumping Spider, White-jawed Jumping Spider, Bald-faced Hornet

Amphibians and Reptiles

In Middlesex County offers a variety of amphibians and reptiles to contribute to the region's ecological diversity. Lakes, Rivers and ponds are inhabited by the Northern Green Frog and Eastern American Toad, while woodlands provide shelter for species like the Eastern Box Turtle and Northern Black Racer.

Green Frog, Spotted Salamander, Eastern Garter Snake, Eastern Musk Turtle

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Middlesex County's various environments houses a diverse range of flora. Wooded areas feature native species like the Red Maple and Eastern Hemlock while meadows boast the colorful blooms of the New England Aster.

Pictures from left to right
Eastern Redcedar, White Snakeroot

Fungi

Fungi in Middlesex County play a key role in recycling nutrients through decomposition and supporting plant health. These species can include chicken of the woods, ringless honey mushrooms, greenshield lichen and more! Preserving these fungi is essential for the overall balance and vitality of the local ecosystems.

Trembling Crust, Coral-Pink Merulius

Environments

Rivers, Creeks and lakes

Middlesex County is traversed by a network of waterways like the Raritan River and Lawrence Brook, along with lakes such as Farrington Lake. These bodies of water sustain diverse aquatic life, from bass in the river to Eastern Brook Trout in the creeks. Apart from their ecological significance, these water bodies also offer recreational opportunities to residents and visitors.

Picture is of the Davidson's Mill Park Pond

Woodlands

Middlesex County features extensive woodlands that contribute significantly to its ecological diversity. Within these wooded areas, native species like the White Oak and American Beech create a thriving ecosystem. The woodlands provide essential habitats for diverse wildlife.

Beaches

While Middlesex does not have many beaches as it only has connection to the Raritan Bay, there are still a few beaches on the coast. Including Clifford Beach Waterfront Park, Old Bridges Waterfront Park and Sadowski Parkway Waterfront Park.

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • Elmer P. Boyd Park - Playground, biking, boat launch and fishing! (fish responsibly)
  • Donaldson Park - Various sports fields, tennis and basketball courts as well as a boat launch and picnic areas!

  • Ireland Brook Conservation - A few enjoyable trails!

  • Jamesburg Park - 1,400 acres of land with over 5 miles of trails!
  • Davidson's Mill Pond Park - A short 2.3 mile trail around Davidsons Millpond
  • Raritan Bay Waterfront Park - Natural areas, sports fields, and bike paths!
  • Old Bridge Waterfront Park - 2 miles of beach, perfect for a sunset!
  • Cheesequake State Park - Over 1,600 acres of park with camp sites, picnic areas, kayak tours, hiking and more!
