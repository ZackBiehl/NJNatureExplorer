Climate

Salem County has a Humid Subtropical Climate. With temperatures ranging from about 86°F in July to about 25°F in january. Nor'eastors, which are strong winds from the north east, are common during the winter and fall and can bring heavy rain, very strong winds, flooding and damage.
The Atlantic Hurricane season typically runs from June 1st to November 30th. However Hurricanes are infrequent in New Jersey as only 15 hurricanes have passed New Jersey since 1850 and few have made landfall. Usually turning into tropical storms before landfall. However, can be devasting for the coast due to potential Storm Surges that can form from the winds moving cyclonically around a storm. Storm surges cause water to overflow onto coastal lands and cause flooding in the area and can result in massive damage, injuries and even deaths

Wildlife

Salem County's diverse landscapes, spanning woodlands, wetlands, and agricultural areas, provide a habitat for a variety of wildlife.

Mammals

Salem County hosts a range of mammals, including white-tailed deer, red foxes, eastern cottontail rabbits, and gray squirrels. These species adapt to different habitats, from wooded areas and agricultural landscapes to wetlands and aquatic environments.

This section excludes marine mammals which can be found in the aquatic and marine life section.

White-footed Mouse, American Mink, White-tailed Deer, American Beaver

Birds

Salem County's birdlife encompasses a variety of species adapting to its woodlands, wetlands, and suburban areas. Common sightings include red-tailed hawks, eastern bluebirds, and red-winged blackbirds and more!

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Eastern Bluebird, Great Blue Heron, Northern Lapwing, Killdeer

Aquatic and Marine Life

Salem County, located along the Delaware River and its connection to the Atlantic Ocean through the river experiences marine influences that contribute to a diverse aquatic ecosystem. The river supports various fish species, including striped bass, catfish, and perch. Additionally, the Delaware Bay is known for hosting marine mammals, and occasional sightings of dolphins add to the rich marine life.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Spot Croaker, Striped Bass, Eastern Mosquitofish, Pantropical Spotted Dolphin

Insects and Arachnids

Salem County hosts a variety of insects and arachnids across its landscapes, with diverse species occupying different niches. In agricultural areas, bees and ants are active, while spiders spin webs in wooded spaces. Wetlands attract dragonflies, and urban environments may house various insects. Salem County's ecosystems support a dynamic community of insects and arachnids.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Variegated Fritillary, Dark Fishing Spider, Blue-winged Scolid, North American Wheel Bug

Amphibians and Reptiles

Salem County sustains a variety of amphibians and reptiles in its diverse habitats. Common sightings include American bullfrogs and painted turtles inhabiting wetland areas, while garter snakes and eastern box turtles find refuge in woodlands and suburban pockets.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Queensnake, Northern Red-bellied Cooter, Fowlers Toad, Four-toed Salamander

Plants and Fungi

Flora

Salem County showcases a diverse array of flora across its landscapes. Deciduous and evergreen trees such as oaks, maples, and pines dominate wooded areas, while wetlands support cattails and various aquatic plants. Agricultural lands also feature crops and wildflowers.

Pictures from left to right starting at top left
Eastern Skunk Cabbage, Striped Wintergreen, Common Reed looking glass, Flat-branched tree-clubmoss

Fungi

Salem County supports a variety of fungi including mushrooms and molds. These organisms play a vital role in nutrient cycling and decomposition within the county's varied landscapes, from woodlands to wetlands. While urbanization may impact some fungal habitats, the presence of natural areas provides environments for a range of fungal species to thrive.

Pictures from left to right
Common Puffball, Candleflame Lichen

Environments

Rivers, Creeks, and Lakes

Salem County is traversed by various rivers and creeks, with the Delaware River being the most significant waterway. The Salem River and Mannington Creek also contribute to the bodies of water in the county. Various lakes in the county also provide homes for many organisms. These water bodies enhance Salem County's natural beauty, provide habitats for aquatic life, and offer recreational opportunities for residents and visitors alike.

Woodlands

Salem County's woodlands serves as great green spaces, from dense forests to pocketed preserves, native trees like oaks and maples provide habitats for wildlife, aswell as offering residents a connection to nature amid the county's mix of urban and rural landscapes.

Parks and Wildlife Reserves

List of Parks and Reserves to Check Out

  • Holly Hills Preserve - Over 2 miles of relaxing trails!
  • Parvin State Park - Over 1,900 acres and trails ranging from 2.5 to 5.4 miles!
  • Mad horse Creek - 9,345 acre area of tidal marsh, great for boating!
  • Abbots Meadow Wildlife Viewing Area - Walk in peace among the salt marshes!
  • Willow Grove Lake Preserve - 2 mile loop through nature!
  • Marlton Recreation Park - Great for kids, has sports facilities, exercise equipment, playgrounds, and a paved track!
  • Camp Kimble, Oldmans Creek Preserve - Beautiful and short hike, about 1.4 miles!
  • Riverview Beach Park - Great spot for sunsets along the Delaware River!
